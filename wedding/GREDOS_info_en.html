Background
 

    La Sierra de Gredos is an impressive mountain range spanning several provinces of central Spain.  The highest point is the Pico Almanzor ( 2,592 m), and Alvaro has climbed this peak with his brother, Pablo.  They have spent many summer vacations here with their father or mother since they were very little, and love it.  If you are able to visit, you will see why!

We will be getting married in a villiage called “Navarredonda de Gredos”, which is 170 km from Madrid.  The town only has 472 registered inhabitants, but many visitors come to this area, particularly in the summer, to enjoy the stunning nature.

Where and what to eat?

 

There are no sophisticated restaurants in Navarredonda, but any bar will offer you delicious tapas, and prices around here are exceptionally low.

Hostal Almanzor offers at noon great tapas and at night our friend Loli cooks delicious meals which Sarah enjoys a great deal; at the Parador you can enjoy local cuisine. El Rincón de Gredos offers simple, delicious local food.

 

Around Navarredonda de Gredos and throughout the province of Ávila you will find free grazing cattle which give a delicious meat. Trout from the river Tormes, migas (a shepherd recipe with dry bread, chorizo, a shot of wine), wild mushrooms (surrounding forests abound with them). Food in this area is genuine, simple and you’ll want to try more!



 

Things to do

 

- In Navarredonda

    Just start walking into the surrounding natural parkland, filled with hills, rivers, and forests.

    Hike up the mountains from the Plataforma, 5 minutes drive from Navarredonda.

 

- Surrounding area

 

- Avila was included in the Unesco World Heritage List in 1985.

 

It is famous for its medieval walls built between the 11th and 14th C.

Home of the prominent Spanish mystic Saint Teresa of Jesus born 5 centuries ago (1515-1582). This year there will be many events celebrating her birth. See here the program.

 

- Segovia, only 70 kms away from Avila is home to one of the best kept over 2000 years old Roman aqueduct, which brought water to the town from the nearby mountains. It is a lively medieval town, where Queen Isabella I of Castile (1451-1504) was crowned and made long stays at the somehow Disney like castle, the Álcazar. The very same queen who financed Columbus first trip to the New World.

It is now home to the Spanish edition of the Hay Festival.

Segovia is also famous for its roasted suckling pig, done in firewood ovens.

 

- Salamanca, The old city of Salamanca was included in the Unesco World Heritage list in 1988. Its university founded in the 1218 is the oldest in Spain and one of the oldest in Europe (Oxford was founded in 1096). Many foreign students come here to study Spanish. Salamanca is a very lively town; its Baroque style main square is one of the most beautiful in Spain.

 

Madrid

 

Capital of Spain. Home of the Prado Museum is not such an overwhelming, monumental city as London, Paris or Rome. But it has a lot of attractive spots. There is always something going on, whether it’s a flamenco show, a Broadway type show, a painting exhibition or a food festival. Enjoy its tapas areas.

Madrid is worth a visit!


All these towns offer wonderful tapas bars, or sophisticated restaurants for foodies!
